# PourSort — Branding & Visual Identity Research

## Summary

PourSort enters one of the most visually saturated sub-genres on the App Store. There are dozens of "Water Sort Puzzle" clones with nearly identical names, identical gameplay, and indistinguishable icons — most featuring a generic tube/bottle on a gradient background. The opportunity is clear: differentiate through deliberate, premium visual identity rather than competing on the same generic terms. This document explores color palette, app icon strategy, typography, and overall aesthetic direction, anchored by the developer's existing brand family (Lumifaste → purple/fasting, AquaFaste → cyan/hydration).

---

## 1. Competitive Landscape — The "Sea of Sameness"

### What the top water-sort icons look like
The App Store's top results for "water sort puzzle" reveal a striking pattern of visual homogeneity:

- **Identical visual formula:** Nearly every icon shows 2–3 test tubes or bottles with bright, saturated liquid layers against a simple gradient background.
- **Color clichés:** Blues, greens, and rainbows dominate. Most use the exact same set of candy-colored liquids.
- **Names blur together:** "Water Sort Puzzle," "Water Sort - Color Puzzle," "Sort Color," "SortPuz" — functionally interchangeable.
- **User pain points:** Reviews frequently mention that similar colors (red vs. dark pink) are hard to distinguish — accessibility is a known weakness across the genre.
- **Ad-heavy, low-trust category:** Reviews are filled with complaints about aggressive ads, fake rewards, broken "remove ads" purchases. The category has a trust problem.

**Implication:** A premium-feeling, ad-respectful brand with distinctive visuals has enormous room to stand out. The bar is low — most competitors look like shovelware.

### Notable differentiation examples outside the category
- **Monument Valley (Ustwo Games):** Proved that a mobile puzzle game can be a design object. Soft color palettes per level, minimalist isometric architecture, every frame designed to be art-worthy. The brand identity is "calm, mysterious, and meditative, with its impossible architecture." Sold 30M+ copies at a premium price.
- **Candy Crush (King):** Saturated, joyful, highly branded. The cascading combos and colorful explosions *are* the brand.
- **Threes!:** Minimal, witty, a personality-driven brand with a small color palette and distinctive character design.

## 2. Color Palette Direction

### Developer Brand Family Context

| App | Primary Color | Domain | Emotional Register |
|-----|--------------|--------|-------------------|
| Lumifaste | Purple (#7C3AED range) | Fasting | Calm, disciplined, spiritual |
| AquaFaste | Cyan (#06B6D4 range) | Hydration | Fresh, clean, flowing |
| PourSort | **TBD** | Puzzle/Game | Playful, satisfying, clever |

The existing apps share a monochromatic-gradient approach — each owned by a single hue. PourSort should continue this one-primary-hue convention for brand consistency, but the game nature demands it feel more playful than the health apps.

### Color Options Analysis

#### Option A: Warm Amber/Orange 🟠
- **Hex range:** #F59E0B → #F97316
- **Rationale:** Warmth, energy, playfulness. Orange conveys optimism and fun. Strongly differentiates from the blue/cyan competition. Complements the existing purple + cyan lineup without overlapping.
- **Risk:** Less "premium" in isolation; needs careful saturation control to avoid looking cheap.
- **Associations:** Creativity, enthusiasm, warmth, friendly competition.

#### Option B: Coral/Warm Pink 🪸
- **Hex range:** #FB7185 → #F43F5E
- **Rationale:** Modern, vibrant, approachable. Coral has trended in design (Pantone 2019 Color of the Year ripple effect). Rare in the water-sort category. Works well with both light and dark backgrounds.
- **Risk:** Could feel too lifestyle/wellness — needs careful pairing to read as "game."
- **Associations:** Joy, energy, contemporary, standout.

#### Option C: Vibrant Teal-to-Lime Gradient 🌊→🍀
- **Hex range:** #14B8A6 → #84CC16
- **Rationale:** Nature-meets-chemistry. The gradient captures the "pouring liquid" metaphor. Fresh, energetic, distinct from AquaFaste's static cyan.
- **Risk:** Could blur with AquaFaste if not handled carefully. Gradient-as-primary is harder to own.

#### Option D: Electric Indigo/Blue-Violet 💜→💙
- **Hex range:** #6366F1 → #8B5CF6
- **Rationale:** Sits between Lumifaste's purple and AquaFaste's cyan — completing a spectral trilogy. Premium feel, modern.
- **Risk:** Too close to Lumifaste. Doesn't differentiate enough within the family.

### Recommendation: **Option A (Amber/Orange)** as primary, with a refined secondary palette

**Primary brand color:** Deep Amber (#D97706) to Warm Orange (#EA580C)
**Rationale:**
1. Maximum differentiation from both sibling apps and the blue-dominated competitor set
2. Warm colors are psychologically associated with energy, fun, and optimism — perfect for a puzzle game
3. Orange/amber is the rarest primary color among water sort competitors (they're all blue/green/rainbow)
4. Creates a visually satisfying brand family: Purple → Cyan → Orange (cool → cool → warm = balanced)

**In-game color palette** (the liquid colors) should be a curated set of 8–12 highly distinguishable hues, tested for:
- Color-blind accessibility (deuteranopia, protanopia, tritanopia)
- Distinctness at small sizes
- Visual harmony when combined in tubes
- Dark mode and light mode contrast

## 3. App Icon Strategy

### What NOT to do (the generic playbook)
- 3 colorful test tubes, straight-on view
- Rainbow liquid layers
- Simple gradient background
- Rounded sans-serif text

Every competitor does this. It's invisible.

### Differentiation Strategies

#### Strategy 1: "The Pour" — Capture the Action
Instead of static tubes, show the **moment of pouring** — a dynamic, mid-flow liquid arc between containers. This communicates gameplay instantly while being visually distinctive. The pour action is the satisfying core mechanic; own it visually.

- Single tube pouring into another, liquid in elegant arc
- Amber/orange brand color as the dominant liquid
- Dark or deep-toned background for contrast
- Subtle motion suggestion through liquid shape

#### Strategy 2: "The Abstraction" — Reduce to Essence
Strip away literal representation. Use abstract flowing shapes or color layers that *suggest* sorting and liquid without literally depicting test tubes. Think along the lines of how Instagram moved from a literal camera to an abstract gradient icon.

- Flowing abstract shape suggesting liquid/pour
- Brand amber as dominant, with 2–3 accent colors
- Clean, modern, would work at any size
- Risks being too abstract for category recognition

#### Strategy 3: "The Premium Minimalist" — One Elegant Vessel
A single, beautifully rendered container (not a generic test tube — perhaps a stylized flask, beaker, or artisan vessel) with perfectly sorted color layers. Quality over quantity. One object, impeccably designed.

- Single vessel, not the usual 2–3 tube arrangement
- Rich amber base with jewel-tone liquid layers
- Subtle depth through soft shadows and highlights
- Reads as "this is a *crafted* game, not shovelware"

### Recommendation: **Strategy 1 + 3 hybrid**

A single elegant vessel with a dynamic pour element. Show one beautifully designed tube mid-pour — the liquid arc captures the action while the single-vessel approach stays clean and premium. The amber brand color should be the background or the dominant liquid color. Keep to 2–3 colors maximum in the icon per current best practices — the icon must work at the smallest home screen size.

Key principles from research:
- Use 2–3 main colors for harmony without clutter
- Focus on a single dominant element rather than complex compositions
- Soft shadows and gradients create depth
- Must remain legible at smallest display size (around 60×60pt on iPhone home screen)
- Design for both light and dark mode contexts

## 4. Typography

### Genre Convention
Puzzle games overwhelmingly use **clean, friendly, rounded sans-serif fonts**. This is well-documented across multiple typography-for-games resources. The convention exists because rounded forms feel approachable and non-threatening — matching the "relaxing brain exercise" positioning.

### Recommendations

#### Display / Logo Font
**Direction:** Rounded geometric sans-serif with personality. Not the generic Montserrat/Roboto that every app uses. Look for:

- **Nunito** or **Nunito Sans** — Rounded terminals, friendly but not childish. Available on Google Fonts with full weight range.
- **Quicksand** — Geometric, rounded, modern. Excellent at display sizes.
- **Rubik** — Slightly rounded corners, geometric, very legible. Has a playful quality without being juvenile.
- **Custom lettering** for the logotype — modify a base font to create a distinctive wordmark where "Pour" and "Sort" have visual differentiation (e.g., "Pour" in a flowing/italic weight, "Sort" in upright/bold).

#### Body / UI Font
**Direction:** Clean, high-legibility sans-serif optimized for small screens.

- **SF Pro** (iOS native) — Best choice for iOS-first development. Dynamic Type support, excellent readability.
- **Inter** — If custom font needed, Inter has exceptional screen readability, tabular numbers for scores/levels, and complete weight range.

#### Hierarchy Pattern
```
Logo/Title:    Rounded geometric display (e.g., Quicksand Bold or custom)
H1/Level:      Same family, semibold, large
Body/UI:       SF Pro (system) or Inter, regular
Numbers:       Tabular figures for level counts, scores
```

Limit to **two font families maximum** — one display, one body. Create hierarchy through weight, size, and color, not additional typefaces.

## 5. Overall Aesthetic Direction

### Brand Personality (5 adjectives)
1. **Satisfying** — the core emotional hook (watching colors sort perfectly)
2. **Clever** — respects the player's intelligence
3. **Warm** — amber palette, inviting, not clinical
4. **Premium** — no ads (or minimal), polished, worth paying for
5. **Fluid** — motion, pour, flow — everything should feel liquid

### Visual Language Principles

1. **Generous whitespace, bold color accents.** Follow the "exaggerated minimalism" trend — clean layouts with oversized, confident UI elements. Not cluttered like the ad-heavy competitors.

2. **Fluid motion design.** Micro-interactions matter enormously in a game about pouring liquid. The pour animation, level complete celebration, and UI transitions should all feel liquid and satisfying. This is where PourSort can dramatically outclass competitors whose animations are "bright but basic."

3. **Dark mode as default (or co-equal).** A deep, warm dark background (not pure black — try #1C1917 warm stone-black) makes the colorful liquids pop dramatically. Many competitors' dark modes are afterthoughts. PourSort should be designed dark-first with a polished light alternative.

4. **Soft, rounded geometry everywhere.** Rounded corners on containers, pill-shaped buttons, smooth curves in the liquid physics. This aligns with the 2025 trend toward soft, rounded edges that "reflect warmth, comfort, and human-centric design."

5. **Sound as brand.** The pour sound, the "click" of a completed tube, the level-complete chime — these are brand elements. Design them deliberately (ASMR-satisfying liquid sounds are already noted as a differentiator in competitor reviews).

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- ❌ Rainbow vomit / too many colors at once in marketing materials
- ❌ Generic gradient backgrounds with no texture or depth
- ❌ Childish / patronizing aesthetic (this is a puzzle for adults too)
- ❌ Any visual similarity to the top 5 water sort apps — differentiation is survival
- ❌ Text on the app icon (violates store policies and becomes illegible)

## 6. Brand Family Coherence

### How the three apps should read together

| Element | Lumifaste | AquaFaste | PourSort |
|---------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Primary Hue | Purple (#7C3AED) | Cyan (#06B6D4) | Amber (#D97706) |
| Mood | Serene, disciplined | Fresh, clean | Warm, playful |
| Category | Health/Fasting | Health/Hydration | Game/Puzzle |
| Icon Style | Monochrome minimal | Monochrome minimal | Colorful minimal |
| Typography | System + clean sans | System + clean sans | Rounded display + system |

The **shared thread:** All three use a single dominant hue as brand anchor, favor clean/minimal UI, and project quality over flashiness. PourSort is allowed to be the most playful of the three because it's a game, but it should still feel like it came from the same studio — a studio that values craft and restraint.

### Naming Observation
"PourSort" follows a different naming convention than the "-Faste" suffix of the other apps. This is likely intentional (it's a game, not a health tool), but if brand family recognition matters, consider the name's relationship to the suite. The "Pour" element is strong — it immediately communicates the mechanic. "Sort" reinforces the puzzle aspect. The compound works well as a distinctive, memorable name in a sea of generic "Water Sort Puzzle" titles.
